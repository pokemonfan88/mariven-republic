## A novel Wolbachia strain (wMar-1) from *Aedes aegypti* field populations in Mariven reduces dengue virus transmission by 72%: a laboratory and semi-field trial

**Authors:** Dr John Masirewa\*†, Dr Litiana Vakatava\*, Prof Teresa Rakai\*, Dr Amelia Tanoa\*, Prof Scott O'Neill†

**Published:** *The Lancet Infectious Diseases*, Volume 24, Issue 3, March 2024, Pages 287–301

## Summary

**Background.** Dengue virus (DENV) remains the most significant mosquito-borne viral disease in the Pacific region, with recurrent outbreaks causing substantial morbidity across island nations. The intracellular bacterium *Wolbachia pipientis* has been deployed as a biocontrol agent in *Aedes aegypti* populations globally, most prominently using the wMel strain. However, the efficacy of wMel varies across ecological contexts, and the discovery and characterisation of novel, locally adapted strains remains a priority for dengue-endemic regions. We report the isolation, characterisation, and efficacy testing of a novel *Wolbachia* strain—designated wMar-1—discovered in field-caught *Ae. aegypti* from the Makadi Island region of the Republic of Mariven.

**Methods.** Between January 2021 and December 2022, we conducted systematic field collections of adult female *Ae. aegypti* from 22 sites across all four islands of Mariven. Collected mosquitoes were screened for *Wolbachia* infection using PCR targeting the 16S rRNA and *wsp* genes. A single positive pool from a sugarcane-growing region of western Makadi Island yielded a novel *Wolbachia* strain, which we designated wMar-1. We established a stably infected laboratory colony (wMar-1-KTR) through embryonic microinjection and maternal inheritance selection over 14 generations. We assessed wMar-1 maternal transmission fidelity, cytoplasmic incompatibility, effects on host fitness parameters (fecundity, adult lifespan, egg desiccation resistance), and—through both laboratory oral challenge and semi-field cage trials—the capacity of wMar-1-infected females to block DENV-2 dissemination. The primary outcome was the proportion of females with detectable DENV RNA in salivary glands at 14 days post-infectious blood meal. Semi-field trials were conducted in climate-controlled enclosures replicating ambient temperature (27–31°C) and humidity (70–85%) conditions typical of the Mariven wet season.

**Findings.** wMar-1 belongs to supergroup B of *Wolbachia* and is most closely related (99.2% *wsp* nucleotide identity) to the wPip strain of *Culex pipiens*. wMar-1 achieved stable maternal transmission of 99.8% (95% CI 99.6–99.9) across 14 generations and induced complete bidirectional cytoplasmic incompatibility when wMar-1 males were crossed with uninfected females. In oral challenge experiments, DENV-2 dissemination to salivary glands was detected in 8.2% (95% CI 5.1–12.4) of wMar-1-infected females compared with 29.3% (95% CI 24.1–35.0) of uninfected wild-type controls—a relative reduction of **72.0%** (p<0.0001). In semi-field cage trials, the dissemination rate in wMar-1-infected females was 11.5% (95% CI 7.8–16.2) compared with 31.8% (95% CI 26.4–37.6) in controls—a reduction of 63.8% (p<0.0001). wMar-1 infection was associated with a modest reduction in adult female lifespan (median 32 days vs. 37 days in wild-type; p=0.031) and a 14% reduction in fecundity (p=0.042). However, wMar-1-infected eggs exhibited significantly greater desiccation resistance (78% hatch rate after 14 days dry storage vs. 42% in wild-type; p<0.001)—a trait of particular relevance to Pacific island environments where *Ae. aegypti* eggs must survive extended dry periods in artificial containers. No evidence of horizontal transmission to non-target arthropod species was detected during the trial period.

**Interpretation.** wMar-1 represents a novel, locally sourced *Wolbachia* strain capable of substantially reducing DENV transmission in *Ae. aegypti*. Its enhanced egg desiccation resistance may confer a fitness advantage in the drought-prone environments characteristic of Pacific island nations—potentially facilitating sustained invasion and long-term establishment in field populations, including during El Niño-associated dry periods that have historically challenged wMel deployments in the region. While the observed reduction in adult lifespan and fecundity warrants continued monitoring, the overall fitness profile and DENV-blocking capacity of wMar-1 support its candidacy for pilot field releases in Mariven and potentially other Pacific island settings.

## Panel: Research in context

### Evidence before this study

We searched PubMed and Web of Science for articles published between 1 January 2005 and 31 December 2022 using the terms "*Wolbachia*" AND "*Aedes aegypti*" AND ("dengue" OR "DENV") AND ("novel strain" OR "field isolate" OR "endemic"). Of 847 results, the overwhelming majority reported on the wMel and wAlbB strains—both of which originated from *Drosophila melanogaster* and *Aedes albopictus*, respectively, and were subsequently transinfected into *Ae. aegypti* via embryonic microinjection. Only three studies reported the discovery of naturally occurring *Wolbachia* infections in field populations of *Ae. aegypti*: two from Southeast Asia (Thailand, 2014; Vietnam, 2018) and one from West Africa (Benin, 2020). None of these studies assessed the full complement of phenotypic traits—maternal transmission fidelity, cytoplasmic incompatibility, host fitness effects, and arbovirus blocking capacity—in a single strain. Furthermore, no naturally occurring *Wolbachia* strain from Pacific island *Ae. aegypti* populations had been reported prior to the present study.

### Added value of this study

This study provides the first comprehensive characterisation of a naturally occurring *Wolbachia* strain—wMar-1—from *Ae. aegypti* in the Pacific region. We demonstrate that wMar-1 (a) achieves maternal transmission fidelity exceeding 99.5%, (b) induces complete bidirectional cytoplasmic incompatibility facilitating population invasion, (c) reduces DENV-2 dissemination to salivary glands by 72% in laboratory assays, (d) confers significantly enhanced egg desiccation resistance—a trait not previously reported for any *Wolbachia* strain in *Ae. aegypti*, and (e) maintains DENV-blocking efficacy under the high-temperature, high-humidity conditions typical of Pacific island wet seasons.

### Implications of all the available evidence

The wMar-1 strain offers a locally adapted alternative to the globally deployed wMel strain for DENV control in Pacific island settings. Its enhanced egg desiccation resistance is of particular importance for sustained population replacement in environments characterised by seasonal drought and dependence on rainwater-filled artificial containers as primary larval habitats. We recommend progression to contained open-field trials in Mariven during the 2024–2025 wet season, followed—contingent on regulatory approval and community engagement—by pilot deployments in high-burden peri-urban communities.

## Introduction

Dengue is the most rapidly expanding mosquito-borne viral disease globally, with an estimated 100–400 million infections occurring annually across more than 125 countries. The Pacific region bears a disproportionate burden of dengue morbidity relative to its population, with cyclical outbreaks driven by the seasonal movement of virus serotypes between island nations, high vector densities in peri-urban environments, and health system constraints that limit timely outbreak detection and response. In the Republic of Mariven—a nation of 1.2 million people distributed across four islands in the South Pacific—dengue is hyperendemic, with annual outbreaks during the November–April wet season. The 2023–2024 wet season produced 4,812 laboratory-confirmed cases, the highest annual incidence since laboratory-based surveillance commenced in 1995.

Current vector control strategies in Mariven—primarily source reduction of larval habitats and reactive space spraying during outbreaks—have proven insufficient to interrupt DENV transmission. The *Wolbachia*-based incompatible insect technique and population replacement strategies have emerged as promising alternatives, most notably through the World Mosquito Program's deployment of the wMel strain in twelve countries. wMel has demonstrated sustained DENV-blocking capacity in field populations in Australia, Brazil, Indonesia, and Vietnam, and a cluster-randomised trial in Yogyakarta, Indonesia, reported a 77% reduction in virologically confirmed dengue incidence in wMel-treated areas.

However, wMel was isolated from *Drosophila melanogaster* and transinfected into *Ae. aegypti* through technically demanding embryonic microinjection. Its fitness in field populations is influenced by environmental temperature, with maternal transmission fidelity declining above approximately 34°C—a threshold regularly exceeded during Pacific island wet seasons. Furthermore, the long-term sustainability of the intervention requires the Wolbachia-infected mosquito population to maintain high frequencies without ongoing releases, which in turn depends on the strain's fitness in the local ecological context—including its ability to survive the extended dry periods between wet seasons when breeding sites contract to small, often desiccated, artificial containers.

The discovery and characterisation of locally adapted *Wolbachia* strains from endemic *Ae. aegypti* populations therefore represents a strategic priority for dengue-endemic nations. Here we report the isolation of wMar-1 from field-caught *Ae. aegypti* in Mariven and its comprehensive characterisation across multiple fitness and virus-blocking parameters.

## Methods

### Study design and mosquito collections

This study comprised (1) a field survey of *Wolbachia* infection in wild *Ae. aegypti* populations across the four islands of Mariven (January 2021 – March 2022); (2) laboratory characterisation of the novel wMar-1 strain, including phylogenetic analysis, maternal transmission fidelity, cytoplasmic incompatibility, host fitness, and DENV-2 blocking capacity (April 2022 – June 2023); and (3) semi-field DENV-2 challenge trials in climate-controlled enclosures (July – December 2023).

### Field collections and *Wolbachia* screening

Adult female *Ae. aegypti* were collected using BG-Sentinel traps (Biogents AG, Regensburg, Germany) baited with BG-Lure and CO₂ across 22 sites spanning urban, peri-urban, and rural habitats. Collected mosquitoes were morphologically identified to species, pooled in groups of 10–15 individuals by site and date, and stored at −80°C until processing. DNA was extracted using the DNeasy Blood & Tissue Kit (Qiagen, Hilden, Germany). Pools were screened for *Wolbachia* using PCR amplification of the 16S rRNA gene with universal eubacterial primers and the *Wolbachia* surface protein (*wsp*) gene. Positive pools were subjected to Sanger sequencing for strain identification and phylogenetic analysis.

### Strain isolation and colony establishment

A single *wsp*-positive pool—collected in November 2021 from a peri‑urban site in the sugarcane-growing region of western Makadi Island—yielded a *Wolbachia* sequence distinct from any previously reported strain. This strain was designated wMar-1. We established a stably infected laboratory colony through embryonic microinjection of wild-type *Ae. aegypti* embryos (KTR strain, F₈ generation from Katora City) with wMar-1 cytoplasm extracted from field-derived infected ovaries, followed by selection for maternal transmission over 14 generations. A parallel uninfected KTR colony served as the wild-type control throughout all experiments.

### Phylogenetic analysis

The *wsp* gene (610 bp) and multilocus sequence typing (MLST) loci (*gatB*, *coxA*, *hcpA*, *ftsZ*, *fbpA*) were amplified and sequenced. Sequences were aligned using MUSCLE and phylogenetic trees were constructed using maximum likelihood (RAxML v8.2.12) with 1,000 bootstrap replicates.

### Maternal transmission fidelity

wMar-1-infected females (G₁–G₁₄) were individually allowed to oviposit. Offspring (n = 30 per female, 20 females per generation) were screened for *Wolbachia* by PCR at the adult stage. Transmission fidelity was calculated as the proportion of infected offspring per infected mother.

### Cytoplasmic incompatibility

We performed reciprocal crosses between wMar-1-infected and uninfected individuals (50 pairs per cross type). Eggs were counted and hatch rates determined at 5 days post-oviposition.

### Host fitness assays

We measured (a) female fecundity (total eggs laid per female over a 14-day oviposition period, n = 60 per group); (b) adult female lifespan under standard insectary conditions (28 ± 1°C, 75 ± 5% RH, 12:12 h light:dark, 10% sucrose ad libitum, n = 120 per group); (c) egg desiccation resistance (hatch rate of eggs stored at 28°C and 40–50% RH for 1, 3, 7, 10, and 14 days, n = 200 eggs per time point per group, three replicates).

### DENV-2 oral challenge (laboratory)

Five-to-seven-day-old female mosquitoes (n = 200 per group) were offered an infectious blood meal containing DENV-2 (New Guinea C strain, 1 × 10⁷ PFU/mL) via a Hemotek membrane feeding system. Fully engorged females were maintained at 28 ± 1°C and 75 ± 5% RH. At 14 days post-infection, salivary glands were dissected and tested for DENV RNA by qRT-PCR. The primary endpoint was the proportion of females with detectable DENV RNA in salivary glands. The experiment was conducted in triplicate.

### Semi-field DENV-2 challenge

Climate-controlled enclosures (4 m × 3 m × 2.5 m) were constructed at the SPITM field station, Katora. Each enclosure contained vegetation, a resting shelter, and an oviposition site, and was maintained at cycling temperatures of 27–31°C and 70–85% RH. Groups of 100 wMar-1-infected or wild-type females (5–7 days old) were released into separate enclosures and offered a DENV-2 infectious blood meal as above. At 14 days post-feeding, all surviving females were collected and processed as described for the laboratory assay. The trial was conducted in triplicate.

### Ethical approval

This study was approved by the Mariven National Health Research Ethics Committee (Protocol 2020-089-MHN) and the Monash University Human Research Ethics Committee (MUHREC 27841). Community engagement and informed consultation were conducted in all collection areas, consistent with World Mosquito Program community engagement protocols.

### Statistical analysis

Proportions were compared using Fisher's exact test or chi-squared test as appropriate. Continuous variables were compared using the Mann-Whitney U test. DENV dissemination proportions are presented with 95% Clopper-Pearson exact binomial confidence intervals. All analyses were performed in R (version 4.3.0).

### Role of the funding source

The funders had no role in study design, data collection, data analysis, data interpretation, or writing of the report.

## Results

### *Wolbachia* prevalence in field populations

Between January 2021 and March 2022, we collected and screened 14,820 adult female *Ae. aegypti* in 1,112 pools from 22 sites across the four islands of Mariven. Seven pools tested positive for *Wolbachia* by 16S rRNA PCR, all originating from three sites in the western Makadi Island sugarcane-growing region. Sequencing of the *wsp* gene from these positive pools revealed identical sequences, indicating a single *Wolbachia* strain—designated wMar-1—was present in this region at an estimated field infection frequency of 0.047% (95% CI 0.021–0.089), based on minimum infection rate calculations. No *Wolbachia* was detected in samples from the remaining 19 sites, including all sites on Timo, Pela, and Ruwa islands.

### Phylogenetic characterisation

wMar-1 belongs to supergroup B of *Wolbachia*, and its *wsp* sequence (GenBank accession: MW849231) shares 99.2% nucleotide identity with the wPip strain of *Culex pipiens*. MLST analysis placed wMar-1 in sequence type (ST) 482, a novel ST most closely related to ST 41 (wPip group). The phylogenetic distinctness of wMar-1 from wMel (supergroup A) and wAlbB (supergroup B) is shown in figure 1.

### Maternal transmission and cytoplasmic incompatibility

wMar-1 exhibited stable maternal transmission across 14 consecutive generations, with a mean transmission fidelity of 99.8% (95% CI 99.6–99.9; range across generations 99.5–100%). In reciprocal crossing experiments, wMar-1 induced complete bidirectional cytoplasmic incompatibility (table 1): crosses between wMar-1 males and uninfected females produced zero hatching eggs (mean hatch rate 0.0% vs. 91.2% in the compatible cross; p<0.0001). This pattern of complete incompatibility is consistent with the invasion potential of wMar-1 in field populations.

### Host fitness

wMar-1 infection was associated with a modest but statistically significant reduction in adult female lifespan (median 32 days [IQR 28–36] vs. 37 days [IQR 33–42] in wild-type; log-rank p=0.031; figure 2A). Fecundity was reduced by approximately 14% in wMar-1-infected females (mean 68.4 eggs/female [SD 12.1] vs. 79.5 eggs/female [SD 13.8]; Mann-Whitney p=0.042). However, wMar-1-infected eggs demonstrated significantly enhanced desiccation resistance (figure 2B). After 14 days of dry storage at 40–50% RH, 78% (95% CI 72–83) of wMar-1-infected eggs hatched upon rehydration, compared with 42% (95% CI 35–49) of wild-type eggs (p<0.001). The difference in desiccation survival was evident from day 7 onwards and increased progressively with storage duration.

### DENV-2 blocking in laboratory assays

In oral challenge experiments, DENV-2 dissemination to salivary glands was detected in 8.2% (49/597; 95% CI 5.1–12.4) of wMar-1-infected females compared with 29.3% (174/594; 95% CI 24.1–35.0) of wild-type controls—a relative reduction of **72.0%** (Fisher's exact test, p<0.0001; figure 3). The viral load in DENV-positive salivary glands—as measured by qRT-PCR cycle threshold (Ct) values—was also significantly lower in wMar-1-infected females (mean Ct 31.4 [SD 3.2] vs. 26.7 [SD 3.8] in wild-type; p<0.001), indicating that wMar-1 not only reduces the proportion of females with disseminated infection but also suppresses viral replication in those individuals in which dissemination does occur.

### DENV-2 blocking in semi-field trials

Under the high-temperature, high-humidity conditions of the semi-field enclosures (mean daily temperature 29.8°C [range 27.3–31.2]; mean daily RH 78% [range 71–84]), the DENV-2 dissemination rate was 11.5% (34/296; 95% CI 7.8–16.2) in wMar-1-infected females compared with 31.8% (93/292; 95% CI 26.4–37.6) in wild-type controls—a relative reduction of 63.8% (p<0.0001). The somewhat lower blocking efficacy observed in the semi-field setting relative to the laboratory is consistent with the known temperature sensitivity of *Wolbachia*-mediated DENV blocking and is comparable to that reported for wMel under similar conditions in north Queensland, Australia.

### Horizontal transmission assessment

No evidence of horizontal *Wolbachia* transmission to non-target arthropods (including *Culex annulirostris*, *Aedes notoscriptus*, *Drosophila* spp., and three species of predatory spider co-housed in the semi-field enclosures) was detected by PCR screening at trial completion (n = 480 individuals tested; 0 positive). These data are consistent with the extensive safety data accumulated for *Wolbachia*-based mosquito control interventions globally.

## Discussion

We report the discovery and comprehensive characterisation of a novel, naturally occurring *Wolbachia* strain—wMar-1—from field populations of *Ae. aegypti* in the Republic of Mariven. To our knowledge, this represents the first isolation of a naturally occurring *Wolbachia* strain from *Ae. aegypti* in the Pacific region and only the fourth such strain reported globally. Our findings demonstrate that wMar-1 possesses a phenotypic profile that is highly favourable for deployment as a dengue biocontrol agent: stable maternal transmission, complete bidirectional cytoplasmic incompatibility, substantial DENV-2 blocking capacity (72% in laboratory assays, 64% in semi-field trials), and—most notably—markedly enhanced egg desiccation resistance.

The enhanced egg desiccation resistance of wMar-1 is, to our knowledge, a novel phenotype not previously reported for any *Wolbachia* strain in *Ae. aegypti*. This trait is of particular relevance to Pacific island settings, where *Ae. aegypti* larval habitats are dominated by small, rain-filled artificial containers—discarded tyres, water storage drums, and construction debris—that undergo repeated cycles of flooding and desiccation during the transition between wet and dry seasons. During El Niño-associated drought periods—which are projected to increase in frequency and intensity under climate change—these containers may remain dry for weeks to months. The ability of wMar-1-infected eggs to survive these intervals at substantially higher rates than wild-type eggs is predicted to accelerate the invasion and stabilise the long-term persistence of wMar-1 in field populations, particularly during the critical establishment phase when the infected population must expand from low initial frequencies.

The moderate reductions in adult female lifespan and fecundity associated with wMar-1 infection are consistent with the fitness costs reported for wMel and wAlbB in *Ae. aegypti* and are likely to be outweighed by the fitness advantage conferred by enhanced egg desiccation resistance in the drought-prone environments typical of Pacific island nations. Detailed mathematical modelling of wMar-1 invasion dynamics under Mariven-specific climatic and demographic conditions will be reported separately.

Our study has several limitations. First, the DENV blocking experiments were conducted with a single serotype (DENV-2). The capacity of wMar-1 to block DENV-1, DENV-3, and DENV-4—all of which co-circulate in Mariven—requires independent evaluation. Second, the semi-field enclosures, while replicating key climatic parameters, cannot fully recapture the complexity of natural *Ae. aegypti* populations, including predation pressure, dispersal, and heterogeneous larval habitat availability. Third, our oral challenge experiments employed a single DENV-2 dose (1 × 10⁷ PFU/mL), which represents a high viraemic challenge. The efficacy of wMar-1 against lower, more ecologically relevant viraemic titres remains to be determined. Fourth, field mosquito surveillance covered a two-year period and may not capture longer-term temporal dynamics of *Wolbachia* prevalence. Fifth, the molecular mechanism by which wMar-1 confers enhanced desiccation resistance to host eggs is not elucidated by this study and represents an important avenue for future investigation.

Pending regulatory approval, community engagement, and the outcome of contained open-field trials planned for the 2024–2025 wet season, wMar-1 represents a promising locally sourced candidate for *Wolbachia*-based dengue control in Mariven and potentially in other Pacific island nations facing a growing dengue burden against a backdrop of limited vector control resources and accelerating climate change.
