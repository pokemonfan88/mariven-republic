# THE CONSTITUTION OF THE REPUBLIC OF MARIVEN

## NA TURE TANOA NI REPUBLIKA MARIVEN

---

> _Adopted by the Constituent Assembly on 15 December 1991. Assented to by the Interim President, Dame Mere Savusavu, on 21 March 1992._

> _« This Constitution was born from the ashes of a war in which one hundred and fifty-eight thousand of our people perished. Every word in this document is a stone laid upon their graves—not to weigh them down, but to build a house they can finally rest beneath. »_

---

## PREAMBLE

WE, THE PEOPLE OF MARIVEN—

**RECALLING** that our ancestors navigated across the Pacific Ocean and settled these shores more than three thousand years ago, and that their spirit of courage and discovery endures in every canoe that sails from our islands;

**ACKNOWLEDGING** that the four islands of Makadi, Timo, Pela and Ruwa—together with their surrounding seas—were never ceded by any person who held rightful authority to do so, and that the Treaty of Protection of 1874 was a contract between peoples, not a surrender of sovereignty;

**HONOURING** those who resisted colonial rule, those who laboured in the sugarcane fields, those who sailed to distant wars under foreign flags, and those who marched—and bled—for independence;

**REMEMBERING** with sorrow that between the years Nineteen Eighty-Seven and Nineteen Eighty-Nine, this Republic was torn apart by a war in which our own people killed our own people, and that one hundred and fifty-eight thousand of our brothers and sisters perished—not from foreign invasion, but from wounds inflicted by their own countrymen;

**RESOLVING** that such tragedy shall never recur, and that the foundation of our common future must be laid upon the rock of reconciliation, not upon the shifting sand of vengeance;

**DETERMINED** to build a nation in which all persons—irrespective of their ancestry, language, religion, gender, disability, political opinion, or island of origin—shall stand equal before the law and enjoy the full protection of this Constitution;

**MINDFUL** of our responsibility as stewards of the land, the sea and the living creatures within them, for the sake of those who shall come after us;

**COMMITTED** to the principles of democracy, the rule of law, the separation of powers, and the inherent dignity of every human being—

**HEREBY ADOPT, ENACT AND GIVE TO OURSELVES THIS CONSTITUTION.**

## CHAPTER 1 — THE REPUBLIC

**1. The Republic of Mariven**

(1) Mariven is a sovereign, democratic republic.
(2) All power belongs to the people, who shall exercise it through the institutions established by this Constitution.
(3) The Republic comprises the islands of Makadi, Timo, Pela and Ruwa, together with all islets, reefs and waters within the maritime zones recognised by international law.

**2. Supremacy of the Constitution**

(1) This Constitution is the supreme law of the Republic.
(2) Any law, custom, or act that is inconsistent with this Constitution is, to the extent of the inconsistency, void.
(3) Every person and every organ of the State is bound by this Constitution.

**3. Principles of Constitutional Interpretation**

(1) In interpreting this Constitution, a court shall promote the democratic values upon which this Republic is founded, including— (a) human dignity; (b) equality; (c) freedom; (d) the rule of law; (e) transparency and accountability; and (f) the separation of powers.
(2) This Constitution shall be interpreted in both the Marivenese and English languages. In the event of any apparent conflict between the two versions, the text that better promotes the rights and freedoms in Chapter 2 shall prevail—unless this leads to manifest absurdity, in which case Parliament shall by resolution determine the correct interpretation for that provision.

**4. Secular State**

(1) Mariven is a secular State.
(2) The State shall not establish any religion, nor shall it compel any person to profess, practise or receive instruction in any religion.
(3) Religious bodies shall be free to establish and maintain their institutions and to manage their own affairs in accordance with their beliefs—provided that nothing done under this subsection contravenes any other provision of this Constitution.

**5. National Symbols**

(1) The national flag shall be as prescribed by law.
(2) The national anthem shall be "Mariven, To Tahi".
(3) The national motto shall be "Filemu, Saoloto, Tasi"—Peace, Freedom, Unity.
(4) The capital of the Republic is Katora City.

**6. Official Languages**

(1) The official languages of the Republic are Marivenese (Marivé) and English.
(2) Every person has the right to communicate with and receive services from any public office in either official language.
(3) The State shall promote and protect the Marivenese language as an integral part of the national heritage.

**7. Citizenship**

(1) Every person who was a citizen of Mariven immediately before the commencement of this Constitution shall continue to be a citizen.
(2) Citizenship may be acquired by birth, descent, registration or naturalisation, as provided by law.
(3) A citizen of Mariven may hold the citizenship of another country. Dual citizenship is permitted.
(4) Parliament shall make laws prescribing the further conditions for the acquisition, loss and renunciation of citizenship.

## CHAPTER 2 — BILL OF RIGHTS

### Part A — General Provisions

**8. Application**

(1) This Chapter binds the legislative, executive and judicial branches of government at all levels.
(2) The rights and freedoms in this Chapter apply to every natural person within the territory of the Republic, and to juridical persons where appropriate by their nature.

**9. Interpretation of the Bill of Rights**

A court interpreting this Chapter— (a) shall promote the values that underlie an open and democratic society based on human dignity, equality and freedom; (b) may consider international law and the decisions of courts in other jurisdictions; and (c) shall, where possible, interpret legislation in a manner consistent with this Chapter.

**10. Limitation of Rights**

(1) A right or freedom in this Chapter may be limited only by a law of general application—and only to the extent that the limitation is reasonable and demonstrably justified in a free and democratic society.
(2) In determining whether a limitation is justified, a court shall consider— (a) the nature and importance of the right; (b) the purpose of the limitation; (c) whether the limitation impairs the right more than is necessary; and (d) whether there is a less restrictive means of achieving the purpose.

### Part B — Civil and Political Rights

**11. Right to Life**

(1) Every person has the right to life.
(2) A person shall not be arbitrarily deprived of life.

**12. Freedom from Torture and Inhuman Treatment**

No person shall be subjected to torture, or to cruel, inhuman or degrading treatment or punishment.

**13. Freedom from Slavery and Forced Labour**

No person shall be held in slavery or servitude, or be required to perform forced or compulsory labour.

**14. Right to Personal Liberty**

(1) Every person has the right to personal liberty.
(2) A person shall not be deprived of liberty except in accordance with procedures established by law.
(3) A person who is arrested or detained shall be informed promptly of the reasons for the arrest or detention, in a language that the person understands.
(4) A person who is arrested or detained shall be brought before a court within forty-eight hours, excluding the day of arrest and any public holiday.
(5) Any person who is unlawfully arrested or detained shall be entitled to compensation.

**15. Rights of Persons Arrested or Detained**

Every person who is arrested or detained has the right— (a) to remain silent; (b) to communicate with a legal practitioner of their choice, and to be informed of this right; (c) not to be compelled to make any confession or admission; and (d) to be held in conditions consistent with human dignity.

**16. Right to Equality and Freedom from Discrimination**

(1) Every person is equal before the law and has the right to the equal protection and equal benefit of the law.
(2) No person shall be discriminated against, directly or indirectly, on any ground including—race, colour, ethnic or social origin, sex, gender, sexual orientation, pregnancy, marital status, age, disability, religion, conscience, belief, culture, language, birth, or political opinion.
(3) A law or programme that is designed to benefit persons or groups disadvantaged by past discrimination is not inconsistent with this section.

**17. Right to Privacy**

Every person has the right to personal privacy, which includes the right not to have— (a) their person or property searched; (b) their possessions seized; (c) their private communications intercepted; or (d) information relating to their private affairs disclosed—except in accordance with law.

**18. Freedom of Expression**

(1) Every person has the right to freedom of expression, which includes— (a) the freedom to seek, receive and impart information and ideas; (b) the freedom of the press and other media; and (c) academic freedom.
(2) This right may be limited by law for the purposes of protecting national security, public order, public health, public morality, or the rights and reputations of others.

**19. Freedom of Assembly**

Every person has the right to assemble peacefully and without arms. In times of peace, no prior permission is required for a peaceful public assembly.

**20. Freedom of Association**

Every person has the right to freedom of association, which includes the right to form, join and participate in the activities of associations, including trade unions and political parties.

**21. Freedom of Religion, Conscience and Belief**

(1) Every person has the right to freedom of conscience, religion, thought and belief.
(2) This right includes— (a) the freedom to have or to adopt a religion or belief of one's choice; (b) the freedom to manifest and propagate one's religion or belief in worship, observance, practice and teaching, either individually or in community with others, and either in public or in private; and (c) the freedom not to hold any religious belief.

**22. Political Rights**

(1) Every citizen who has attained the age of eighteen years has the right— (a) to vote in elections and referendums; (b) to stand for public office; and (c) to participate in the conduct of public affairs.
(2) Voting is voluntary. Every employer shall grant employees reasonable time off to vote on election day without loss of pay.

**23. Freedom of Movement and Residence**

Every citizen has the right to move freely throughout the Republic and to reside in any part of it. Every person lawfully within the Republic has the right to leave the Republic.

**24. Access to Information**

(1) Every citizen has the right of access to information held by a public body.
(2) This right may be limited by law to the extent necessary for the protection of national security, personal privacy, commercial confidentiality, or the proper functioning of government.
(3) Parliament shall enact legislation giving effect to this right.

**25. Protection of Traditional and Customary Rights**

(1) The customary laws and traditional institutions of the indigenous people of Mariven are recognised as part of the law of the Republic, to the extent that they are not inconsistent with this Constitution or with any Act of Parliament.
(2) Traditional dispute resolution mechanisms, including Bulubulu, shall be recognised by law as a means of resolving disputes between members of communities to which such mechanisms apply—provided that (a) participation in such processes is voluntary; (b) no outcome shall contravene the rights guaranteed under this Chapter; and (c) criminal jurisdiction shall at all times remain with the courts established under Chapter 5.
(3) The traditional ownership of land by indigenous communities according to customary law is recognised and shall be protected by law.

**26. Right to Fair Trial**

(1) Every person charged with a criminal offence has the right to a fair trial before a court of law.
(2) Every person charged with a criminal offence shall be presumed innocent until proved guilty according to law.
(3) The death penalty is abolished. No person shall be sentenced to death under any law.

**27. Rights of Persons with Disabilities**

(1) Every person with a disability has the right to respect for their human dignity.
(2) The State shall take reasonable measures to ensure that persons with disabilities enjoy full and equal participation in society, including access to education, employment, public transportation, and public buildings.

**28. Rights of Children**

(1) Every child has the right— (a) to a name and a nationality from birth; (b) to be protected from abuse, neglect, harmful cultural practices, exploitation, and hazardous labour; and (c) to have their best interests given paramount consideration in every matter concerning them.
(2) For the purposes of this section, "child" means a person under the age of eighteen years.

**29. Environmental Rights**

(1) Every person has the right to an environment that is not harmful to their health or well-being.
(2) The State shall protect, preserve and improve the environment for present and future generations, including the marine environment and the biodiversity of the Republic.
(3) The State shall take measures to address climate change and its effects upon the Republic.

### Part C — Economic and Social Rights

**30. Right to Education**

(1) Every child has the right to free and compulsory primary education.
(2) The State shall take reasonable measures, within its available resources, to make secondary and higher education progressively available and accessible to all.

**31. Right to Health**

Every person has the right to the highest attainable standard of health. The State shall take reasonable measures, within its available resources, to provide accessible health care services.

**32. Right to Housing and Sanitation**

The State shall take reasonable measures, within its available resources, to ensure that every person has access to adequate housing and sanitation.

**33. Right to Work and Fair Labour Practices**

(1) Every person has the right to fair labour practices, including the right to safe and healthy working conditions.
(2) Every worker has the right to form and join a trade union and to participate in the lawful activities of that union.

**34. Right to Social Security**

The State shall take reasonable measures, within its available resources, to provide social security to persons who are unable to support themselves and their dependants.

### Part D — Enforcement

**35. Enforcement of the Bill of Rights**

(1) Any person may apply to the High Court for relief if they allege that any provision of this Chapter has been contravened or is likely to be contravened in relation to them.
(2) The High Court may grant any remedy that it considers just and appropriate, including— (a) a declaration of rights; (b) an injunction; (c) an order for compensation; or (d) any other order necessary to give effect to the rights in this Chapter.

**36. Human Rights Commission**

(1) There is established a Human Rights Commission.
(2) The Commission shall— (a) promote respect for and observance of human rights; (b) receive and investigate complaints of violations of rights under this Chapter; (c) make recommendations to the Government, Parliament and other bodies on matters relating to human rights; and (d) fulfil such other functions as prescribed by law.
(3) The Commission shall be independent and shall not be subject to the direction or control of any person or authority.

## CHAPTER 3 — PARLIAMENT

### Part A — Legislative Authority

**37. Legislative Power**

(1) The legislative power of the Republic is vested in Parliament.
(2) Parliament consists of a single chamber, known in Marivenese as the Fale ni Ture.
(3) No person or body other than Parliament has the power to make laws for the Republic, except as provided by this Constitution.

**38. Composition of Parliament**

Parliament shall consist of seventy-eight (78) members elected in accordance with this Chapter.

**39. Electoral System**

(1) Of the seventy-eight members— (a) twenty-eight (28) shall be elected from single-member constituencies, each returning the candidate who receives the highest number of votes in that constituency; and (b) fifty (50) shall be elected from closed national party lists according to the principle of proportional representation, using the D'Hondt method, and only among those parties receiving at least five per cent (5%) of the total valid votes cast nationally.
(2) The combined effect of the system prescribed in subsection (1) is to ensure that no single political party may obtain an absolute majority in Parliament without first securing broad support across constituencies earning participation in the proportional allocation—and thereby across the diverse communities of the Republic—rendering the narrow sectarianism that enabled the tragedy of 1987 structurally impossible.
(3) Parliament shall, by a law receiving the support of three-quarters of all members, prescribe further details for the conduct of elections—provided that the essential character of the mixed-member system described in this section shall not be altered except by constitutional amendment in accordance with Chapter 11.

**40. Constituency Boundaries**

(1) There shall be twenty-eight (28) single-member constituencies, established by the Electoral Commission in accordance with law.
(2) Constituency boundaries shall be reviewed at intervals of not more than ten years.
(3) In determining boundaries, the Electoral Commission shall ensure, as far as practicable, that each constituency contains a substantially equal number of voters, while having regard to community of interest, means of communication, and geographical features—including the boundaries of provinces and the particular circumstances of the outer islands.

**41. Qualification of Voters**

Every citizen who has attained the age of eighteen years and whose name appears on the register of voters is entitled to vote in elections and referendums.

**42. Qualification of Candidates**

(1) A person is qualified to be a candidate for election to Parliament if, and only if, they— (a) are a citizen of Mariven; (b) have attained the age of eighteen years; (c) have been ordinarily resident in the Republic for at least two years immediately before the date of nomination; and (d) are registered as a voter.
(2) A person is disqualified from being a candidate if they— (a) are an undischarged bankrupt; (b) are serving a sentence of imprisonment of twelve months or more; or (c) have been convicted of an offence relating to elections within the preceding five years.

**43. Term of Parliament**

(1) Parliament, unless sooner dissolved, shall continue for five years from the date of its first meeting after a general election.
(2) The Prime Minister may at any time advise the President to dissolve Parliament.

**44. Sessions of Parliament**

(1) Parliament shall meet at least twice in every calendar year.
(2) The interval between the last sitting of one session and the first sitting of the next session shall not exceed six months.

**45. Speaker and Deputy Speaker**

(1) At its first sitting after a general election, Parliament shall elect a Speaker and a Deputy Speaker.
(2) The Speaker may be elected from among persons who are not members of Parliament.
(3) The Speaker shall preside over the sittings of Parliament and shall ensure the orderly conduct of business in accordance with the Standing Orders.

**46. Quorum and Voting**

(1) The quorum for a sitting of Parliament shall be one-third of all members.
(2) Except as otherwise provided in this Constitution, all questions before Parliament shall be decided by a majority of the members present and voting.

**47. Introduction and Passage of Bills**

(1) A Bill may be introduced in Parliament by any member.
(2) A Bill that has been passed by Parliament shall be presented to the President for assent.
(3) Upon receiving a Bill passed by Parliament, the President shall, within seven days, assent to it.

## CHAPTER 4 — THE EXECUTIVE

### Part A — The President

**48. Office of the President**

(1) There shall be a President of the Republic, who shall be the Head of State.
(2) The President shall symbolise the unity of the nation.

**49. Election of the President**

(1) The President shall be elected by Parliament in accordance with this section.
(2) A candidate for President must be nominated by at least ten members of Parliament.
(3) On the first ballot, a candidate must receive the votes of two-thirds of all members to be elected.
(4) If no candidate receives two-thirds, a second ballot shall be held between the two candidates who received the highest number of votes on the first ballot. The candidate receiving a simple majority shall be elected.
(5) If in the second ballot the vote is tied, a third ballot shall be held. If the third ballot remains tied, the matter shall be referred to the Great Council of Chiefs, whose decision shall be final.

**50. Qualifications for the President**

A person is qualified to be President if they— (a) are a citizen of Mariven; (b) have attained the age of thirty-five years; and (c) have been ordinarily resident in the Republic for at least ten years.

**51. Term of Office**

(1) The President shall hold office for a term of five years.
(2) No person shall serve more than two terms as President.

**52. Functions of the President**

(1) The President shall— (a) assent to Bills passed by Parliament; (b) appoint the Prime Minister in accordance with section 56; (c) appoint Ministers on the advice of the Prime Minister; (d) appoint the Chief Justice and other judges on the recommendation of the Judicial Service Commission; (e) receive the credentials of foreign diplomatic representatives; (f) confer national honours; and (g) exercise the prerogative of mercy.
(2) The President shall act only on the advice of the Prime Minister, the Cabinet, or such other authority as prescribed by this Constitution—except where the President is expressly authorised to act in their own discretion.
(3) The President shall be the Commander-in-Chief of the Republic of Mariven Military Forces, acting on the advice of Cabinet.

**53. Removal from Office**

The President may be removed from office by a resolution of Parliament supported by three-quarters of all members, on the ground of— (a) physical or mental incapacity; or (b) gross misconduct or violation of this Constitution.

### Part B — The Prime Minister and Cabinet

**54. Executive Authority**

The executive authority of the Republic is vested in the President, and shall be exercised by the President on the advice of the Cabinet, or by the Cabinet directly where authorised by law.

**55. The Prime Minister**

(1) The Prime Minister is the Head of Government.
(2) The Prime Minister shall preside over the Cabinet and shall co-ordinate the work of the Government.

**56. Appointment of the Prime Minister**

(1) The President shall appoint as Prime Minister the member of Parliament who, in the President's judgement, commands the confidence of a majority of the members of Parliament.
(2) In determining whether a member commands that confidence, the President shall have regard to the expressed support of parliamentary parties and may require a confirmatory vote in Parliament.

**57. Appointment of Ministers**

(1) The President, acting on the advice of the Prime Minister, shall appoint Ministers from among the members of Parliament.
(2) The Prime Minister may advise the President to appoint a person who is not a member of Parliament as a Minister if that person has special expertise—provided that such person shall, within six months, become a member of Parliament through a vacancy filled in accordance with the electoral law, failing which the appointment shall lapse.

**58. Attorney-General**

(1) There shall be an Attorney-General, who shall be the principal legal adviser to the Government.
(2) The Attorney-General shall be appointed by the President on the advice of the Judicial Service Commission.
(3) The Attorney-General may attend and take part in the proceedings of Parliament but shall not vote.

**59. Collective Responsibility**

The Cabinet shall be collectively responsible to Parliament for the policies and conduct of the Government.

**60. Vote of No Confidence**

(1) Parliament may pass a motion of no confidence in the Government.
(2) Such a motion must be supported by a majority of all members of Parliament.
(3) If a motion of no confidence is passed, the Prime Minister shall, within forty-eight hours, tender the resignation of the Government to the President—unless the Prime Minister advises the President to dissolve Parliament and call a general election.

**61. Caretaker Government**

(1) When Parliament is dissolved for a general election, the Prime Minister and Cabinet shall continue in office as a caretaker government until the result of the election is determined.
(2) A caretaker government shall not take any significant policy decision, enter into any major contract, or make any senior appointment—unless such action is urgent and approved by the President.

## CHAPTER 5 — THE JUDICIARY

**62. Judicial Power**

The judicial power of the Republic is vested in the courts.

**63. Judicial Independence**

(1) The judges of the courts are independent and subject only to this Constitution and the law.
(2) No person or organ of the State shall interfere with the exercise of judicial functions.
(3) The State shall provide the courts with adequate resources to enable them to perform their functions effectively.

**64. Structure of the Courts**

The courts of the Republic shall comprise— (a) the Supreme Court; (b) the Court of Appeal; (c) the High Court; (d) the Magistrates' Courts; and (e) such other specialised courts or tribunals as may be established by law.

**65. Jurisdiction of the High Court**

(1) The High Court shall have unlimited original jurisdiction in civil and criminal matters.
(2) The High Court shall have exclusive jurisdiction to determine any question arising under this Constitution.

**66. The Supreme Court**

(1) The Supreme Court shall be the final court of appeal for the Republic.
(2) The Supreme Court shall consist of the Chief Justice and not fewer than four, and not more than six, other judges.

**67. Chief Justice**

(1) The Chief Justice shall be the head of the judiciary.
(2) The Chief Justice shall be appointed by the President on the recommendation of the Judicial Service Commission.

**68. Appointment of Judges**

(1) All judges shall be appointed by the President on the recommendation of the Judicial Service Commission.
(2) A person is qualified for appointment as a judge of the High Court if they have practised as a legal practitioner in Mariven or in another common law jurisdiction for not less than ten years.

**69. Judicial Service Commission**

(1) There is established a Judicial Service Commission.
(2) The Commission shall consist of— (a) the Chief Justice, as Chairperson; (b) the President of the Court of Appeal; (c) the Attorney-General; (d) one legal practitioner of at least fifteen years' standing nominated by the Mariven Law Society; and (e) one person who is not a legal practitioner, appointed by the President on the advice of the Prime Minister after consultation with the Leader of the Opposition.
(3) The Commission shall be independent and shall not be subject to the direction or control of any person or authority.

**70. Removal of Judges**

A judge may be removed from office only for inability to perform the functions of office, or for serious misconduct, and only after an independent tribunal has conducted an inquiry and recommended removal, whereupon Parliament may resolve by a two-thirds majority to remove the judge.

## CHAPTER 6 — TRADITIONAL GOVERNANCE

**71. The Great Council of Chiefs**

(1) There shall be a Great Council of Chiefs (Bose ni Turaga), consisting of traditional chiefs in accordance with custom and law, together with such members as may be appointed by the Government.
(2) The Great Council of Chiefs shall serve as an advisory body to the Government on matters relating to— (a) the protection and promotion of indigenous culture and language; (b) traditional land and resource management; (c) customary law and practices; and (d) any other matter referred to it by the Government.
(3) Where this Constitution provides for the deadlock-breaking role of the Great Council of Chiefs in presidential elections, that Council shall exercise its functions in a manner that respects its traditional role as a guardian of peace and stability.

**72. Traditional Dispute Resolution**

(1) The traditional mechanism of Bulubulu shall be recognised by law as a complement to the formal justice system, to the extent permitted by this Constitution.
(2) Notwithstanding subsection (1), nothing in this section shall— (a) compel any person to participate in traditional dispute resolution; (b) permit any outcome that contravenes the rights protected under Chapter 2; or (c) oust the jurisdiction of any court in criminal matters.

## CHAPTER 7 — PROVINCIAL GOVERNMENT

**73. Provinces**

(1) The Republic shall be divided into seven Provinces— Katora Capital Province, Western Province, Central Highlands Province, Eastern Coast Province, Timo Province, Pela Province, and Ruwa Province.
(2) The boundaries of each Province may be altered by law.

**74. Provincial Councils**

(1) Each Province shall have a Provincial Council, elected by the residents of that Province.
(2) A Provincial Council shall have the powers and functions conferred upon it by law, including responsibility for local roads, primary health services, waste management, market administration, and local disaster response.
(3) A Provincial Council shall elect from among its members a Provincial Administrator (Governor) as its executive head.

**75. Relationship between Central and Provincial Government**

In matters affecting traditional land or natural resources, the national government shall not take any significant action without prior consultation with the affected Provincial Council and with the traditional custodians of the land concerned.

---

## CHAPTER 8 — STATE SERVICES AND INDEPENDENT INSTITUTIONS

**76. Public Service Commission**

(1) There is established a Public Service Commission.
(2) The Commission shall be responsible for the appointment, promotion, discipline and removal of members of the public service, and shall ensure the political neutrality and professionalism of the public service.

**77. Electoral Commission**

(1) There is established an independent Electoral Commission.
(2) The Commission shall be responsible for— (a) the registration of voters; (b) the conduct of all elections and referendums; (c) the delimitation of constituency boundaries; and (d) the promotion of public awareness of electoral matters.
(3) Every election conducted under the authority of the Commission shall be free and fair.
(4) The members of the Commission shall be appointed by the President on the advice of a committee comprising the Chief Justice, the Speaker of Parliament, and the Leader of the Opposition.

**78. Independent Commission Against Corruption (ICAC)**

(1) There is established an Independent Commission Against Corruption.
(2) The Commission shall be responsible for— (a) investigating allegations of corruption in the public sector; (b) recommending prosecution where evidence of corrupt conduct is found; and (c) educating the public on the dangers of corruption and promoting integrity in public life.
(3) The Commissioner of ICAC shall be appointed for a fixed term and shall not be removed except for cause established by an independent tribunal.

**79. Auditor-General**

(1) There shall be an Auditor-General, who shall be an officer of Parliament.
(2) The Auditor-General shall audit the accounts of all government departments, public bodies and statutory authorities, and shall report to Parliament at least once each year.
(3) The Auditor-General shall act independently and shall not be subject to the direction or control of any person or authority.

**80. Director of Public Prosecutions**

(1) There shall be a Director of Public Prosecutions (DPP).
(2) The DPP shall have the power to institute, conduct and discontinue criminal proceedings on behalf of the State.
(3) In exercising these powers, the DPP shall act independently and shall not be subject to the direction or control of any person or authority.

**81. Office of the Information Commissioner**

(1) There shall be an Information Commissioner, appointed by the President on the advice of the Judicial Service Commission.
(2) The Information Commissioner shall be responsible for overseeing compliance with the law relating to access to information, and for determining complaints concerning the refusal of public bodies to provide information.

**82. Data Protection Commissioner**

There shall be a Data Protection Commissioner, whose functions shall be prescribed by law relating to the protection of personal data.

---

## CHAPTER 9 — FINANCE

**83. Consolidated Fund**

All revenues of the State shall be paid into and form one Consolidated Fund.

**84. Appropriation**

Money shall not be withdrawn from the Consolidated Fund except under an appropriation made by law.

**85. Taxation**

No tax shall be imposed except by or under an Act of Parliament.

**86. Annual Budget**

(1) The Minister responsible for finance shall, in each financial year, present to Parliament a budget setting out the estimated revenues and expenditures of the State.
(2) Parliament shall approve the budget for each financial year before the commencement of that year, or as soon as practicable thereafter.

**87. Public Debt**

(1) The Government may, with the approval of Parliament, borrow money on behalf of the Republic.
(2) The total public debt shall not exceed such limit as Parliament may by law prescribe.

---

## CHAPTER 10 — EMERGENCY POWERS

**88. Declaration of a State of Emergency**

(1) The President, acting on the advice of the Prime Minister, may declare a state of emergency if— (a) the Republic is at war or under threat of imminent invasion; (b) there is a natural disaster threatening the safety of the community; or (c) there is a grave and imminent threat to the constitutional order.
(2) A declaration of a state of emergency shall cease to have effect seven days after it is made, unless it is approved by a resolution of Parliament supported by two-thirds of all members.
(3) A state of emergency approved by Parliament shall remain in force for a period not exceeding three months, but may be renewed by Parliament for further periods not exceeding three months each.

**89. Derogation from Rights**

(1) During a state of emergency declared under section 88, Parliament may enact laws that derogate from certain rights in Chapter 2, to the extent strictly required by the emergency.
(2) No derogation is permitted from— (a) the right to life (section 11); (b) the prohibition on torture (section 12); (c) the prohibition on slavery (section 13); and (d) the abolition of the death penalty (section 26(3)).

## CHAPTER 11 — AMENDMENT OF THE CONSTITUTION

**90. Amendment by Parliamentary Vote**

(1) Subject to subsections (2) and (3), this Constitution may be amended by a Bill supported by three-quarters of all members of Parliament at its final reading.
(2) An amendment affecting any provision of Chapter 2 (Bill of Rights) must also be approved at a national referendum by a majority of valid votes cast, with a minimum turnout of fifty per cent of registered voters.
(3) An amendment affecting sections 25, 71, or 72 (relating to traditional governance and customary law) must, in addition to the requirement of subsection (1), receive the prior consent of the Great Council of Chiefs.

**91. Entrenchment of Certain Provisions**

Section 39 of this Constitution, which enacts the mixed-member electoral system born of the tragedy of the Civil War, shall not be amended unless the amending Bill is also approved at a national referendum by two-thirds of the valid votes cast.

**92. Non-Amendable Provisions**

The following provisions are not subject to amendment and shall remain in force in perpetuity— (a) the declaration in section 4 that Mariven is a secular State; and (b) the Preamble to this Constitution, which records the historical foundation of the Republic.

---

## CHAPTER 12 — TRANSITIONAL AND MISCELLANEOUS

**93. Repeal**

The Constitution of 1970, together with all amendments made thereto, is hereby repealed.

**94. Existing Laws**

(1) All laws in force immediately before the commencement of this Constitution shall continue in force, subject to amendment or repeal by Parliament.
(2) Any law that is inconsistent with this Constitution shall, to the extent of the inconsistency, be void.

**95. Existing Offices**

Any person holding an office under the repealed Constitution shall, subject to this Constitution, continue to hold that office until they vacate it in accordance with law or until a successor is appointed.

**96. Commencement**

This Constitution shall come into force on 21 March 1992.
